A window seat with built-in storage drawers is one of those home features that makes you feel like you live in a much more considered, well-designed space. The one pictured here — a wide oak bench with three deep drawers, a thick cushion in cool grey, and decorative pillows — sits in a garden-facing window with sheer curtains and afternoon light pouring in. Beside it, a bookshelf with plants. In front, a hint of a rug. It is everything a reading nook should be.

## Why window seats work

A window seat solves three problems simultaneously: it gives you somewhere to sit that isn't the sofa (important for a sense of variety in how you use a room), it stores things that would otherwise have no home (the drawers here could hold books, throws, board games or seasonal items), and it makes a window into an architectural feature rather than just a hole in the wall.

## Building or buying a window seat

Custom built-in window seats are the best version but require carpentry skill or a tradesperson. A simpler approach: buy a low bookcase or storage bench of the right depth, position it under the window, and add a custom-cut cushion on top. The result looks almost as good for a fraction of the cost.

## Choosing the cushion

A thick cushion — at least 8–10 cm deep — is what makes a window seat genuinely comfortable rather than decorative. Choose a fabric that wipes clean (boucle and performance linen are popular choices), in a colour that works with your wall and curtain tones. Shades of grey, sage, dusty blue and warm cream are the most versatile.

## The plants and bookshelf beside it

The bookshelf to the right of the window seat is a perfect companion: books to read, plants at different heights, a ceramic pot or two. Together they reinforce the sense that this is a deliberate, loved corner rather than a leftover space.

## Interior tips

- **Use sheer curtains** rather than blackout blinds at a window seat — the point is the light, and sheers filter it beautifully without blocking it.
- **Add a throw blanket** draped casually over one end of the cushion — it signals that this is a place to relax and adds warmth to the corner.
- **Pull-out drawers are more practical than lift-up lids** in a window seat because you can access them without moving the cushion.
- **Keep the pillows simple**: two or three in different textures and a coordinating pattern. Too many cushions make sitting down an exercise in rearranging.
- **A small side table** nearby — for a cup of tea, a phone and a candle — completes the reading nook and makes it actually usable.

## DIY vs. professional installation

A window seat with drawers is one of the most achievable DIY projects for a competent home improver, but it's worth understanding what you're taking on before you start.

The simplest approach — and the one that works well in rented homes — is the "furniture stack" method: buy a low storage unit (like IKEA's KALLAX or similar cube shelving on its side), position it under the window, and top it with a custom-cut foam cushion wrapped in your chosen fabric. This takes a weekend and costs a fraction of a custom build.

For a fully built-in look with flush drawers and a seamless painted finish, you'll need either solid carpentry skills or a joiner. The cost varies widely, but expect to pay £500–£1,500 for a professional carpenter to build a bespoke window seat with three to four drawers, depending on size and material. The result is worth it — a built-in window seat adds perceived value to a home in a way that freestanding furniture never quite matches.

## Making the most of your reading nook

A window seat only functions as a reading nook if the surrounding environment supports it. The shelf beside the window in this image is key: accessible books, a plant at eye height, something small and decorative. The subconscious message is that this is a place for slow, quiet activity — and that message encourages you to actually use it that way.

Consider the following additions to make the nook feel complete:

- **A clip-on or adjustable arm reading light** for evening use, positioned to light the page without casting glare on the window
- **A small wicker basket or tray** on the seat itself for glasses, bookmarks and hand cream — the small things that make a reading session comfortable
- **Curtains that can be drawn**, to cocoon the nook on rainy or dark afternoons and increase the sense of enclosure and warmth

## What to store in the drawers

Deep storage drawers under a window seat are surprisingly generous in capacity. The most practical uses are:

- Extra bedding (duvets, spare pillows) — the single most common use and very effective
- Out-of-season clothing in vacuum storage bags
- Children's toys and games in labelled fabric boxes
- Books that overflow from nearby shelving
- Sports or hobby equipment (yoga mat, rolled-up maps, craft supplies)

The important thing is to assign each drawer a category and stick to it. A window seat with drawers full of random items becomes a problem to open rather than a pleasure.

## Common mistakes when building a window seat

**Choosing the wrong cushion depth.** A 4 cm cushion feels like sitting on a shelf. Spend more on a foam cushion that's at least 8 cm deep — 10 cm is even better. Dense foam (35–40 kg/m³) holds its shape and doesn't bottom out the way cheap foam does.

**Ignoring the reveal.** If there's a window reveal — the recessed wall space between the window frame and the room — build the seat to fit inside it exactly. A seat that's slightly too narrow for the reveal looks provisional; one that fills it precisely looks architectural.

**Making it too perfect to sit on.** A window seat loaded with perfectly arranged cushions that have to be rearranged before you can sit is a window seat no one actually uses. Keep it simple: one cushion base, two or three pillows, one throw.
