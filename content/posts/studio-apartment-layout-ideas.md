Viewed from above and at an angle, this studio apartment reveals a layout that is almost architectural in its clarity. In the foreground: a cream sofa with navy cushions, a navy throw draped across the arm. To the left: a dark wooden desk positioned against the large window, a laptop open, a wall sconce on either side, a fiddle leaf fig in the corner, a large framed landscape print above the desk. Centered in the room: a speckled cream rug with a slatted wooden privacy panel beside it. Beyond: a single bed with cream bedding and one navy cushion. A small round dining table with two white chairs and a pendant light above. To the right: a full-height wall of light oak built-in shelving and cabinetry with a round mirror, a console table and plants. In the background: a dark wood kitchen with a dome pendant. This apartment has five distinct zones — and from this angle, every one is visible simultaneously.

## The home office by the window

The desk is positioned to make maximum use of the apartment's best natural light. Two wall sconces flank the space above the desk, providing warm light for evening work. The large framed print above the desk and the fiddle leaf fig to the left make this workspace feel considered rather than improvised — it is a place you'd want to sit, not just a surface with a laptop on it. Placing the workspace at the window also means the person at the desk looks out, not at a wall.

## The slatted wooden divider

The vertical slatted wooden panel standing between the sofa and the sleeping area is the room's most interesting detail. It's not a full wall and not a shelf — it's a screen, providing visual separation between zones without blocking light or creating a full barrier. The vertical slats create rhythm and shadow play, and the natural wood tone ties into the desk and the built-in shelving on the opposite wall.

## The built-in wall

The full-height oak shelving and cabinetry along the right wall is the room's most significant storage element. Open shelves hold books, plants and objects; closed cabinets hold everything that doesn't need to be on display. A round mirror near the bottom reflects the room and provides a dressing moment without requiring a separate dressing area. A console table below the mirror creates a surface for keys, ceramics and a lamp.

## Navy as the accent colour

Throughout the room, navy blue appears consistently but sparingly: one cushion on the sofa, one on the bed, the throw on the sofa arm. This restraint is exactly right. Navy against cream and light oak is a classic pairing that reads as confident without being loud. Three appearances of the same colour — in different forms, at different scales — is enough to read as a deliberate palette.

## Interior tips

- **Put the workspace at the window**: this is the single best decision you can make in a studio apartment. The person who works from home needs the most light; the window gives the most light. It's that simple.
- **A slatted wooden panel creates privacy without darkness**: the gaps between the slats allow light to pass through while creating visual separation. It performs the function of a wall at a fraction of the visual weight.
- **Use one accent colour in three places only**: the navy blue here appears exactly three times. More and it becomes the theme; fewer and it reads as accidental. Three is the number that says "deliberate."
- **A round dining table for two seats four comfortably**: a small round table in a studio can seat two people every day and four people when needed. It's the most versatile dining footprint for a single resident.
- **Full-height built-ins eliminate the need for multiple smaller storage pieces**: one floor-to-ceiling wall of storage replaces a wardrobe, a bookcase, a console table and a dresser. The visual result is cleaner, and the storage is more generous than all four separate pieces combined.
