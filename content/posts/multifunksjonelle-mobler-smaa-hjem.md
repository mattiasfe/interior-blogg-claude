This nook is everything a reading corner should be: enclosed, warm, completely separate from the rest of the room, and deeply, irresistibly comfortable. A hanging hammock chair woven from natural rope fills the alcove, loaded with white cushions in linen and textured cotton — a sheepskin, a rust-brown leather cushion, a macramé pillow. Above, two thick reclaimed wood floating shelves hold books and a few decorative objects. A globe pendant light on a copper fitting hangs at eye level. A trailing plant spills from a hook on the left. On the floor: a vintage patterned rug in muted colours. This nook has every element of the perfect reading space.

## Why a hanging chair works where a regular chair doesn't

A hanging chair does something a floor-standing chair cannot: it floats. The slight movement, the give of the ropes, the way your body settles into the woven base — it's immediately distinctive. More practically, it takes up very little floor space and can be installed in a corner, an alcove or even a staircase landing that wouldn't accommodate a full armchair. And visually, it's a statement that a chair simply isn't.

## Installing a hammock chair safely

A hanging chair needs a strong anchor point — either a ceiling joist (not plasterboard alone) or a dedicated wall bracket. The chair and occupant combined can weigh over 100 kg, so this is a structural decision, not a decorative one. Use a heavy-duty ceiling hook with a load rating of at least 150 kg, a short length of steel chain, and a carabiner to allow easy height adjustment. If you're unsure about your ceiling, consult a builder before installing.

## The bookshelf wall

The two floating shelves here are wide, deep and made from reclaimed or rustic timber with a live edge. They're installed close enough together to suggest a library without requiring a full built-in bookcase. The styling is deliberately mixed: books spine-out rather than cover-out, a framed print leaning rather than hung, a candle, a small vase. Nothing is too ordered — this is a reading nook, not a showroom.

## Light: the globe pendant

The pendant light hanging at eye level above the chair is the single most important lighting choice in this nook. A globe bulb in a clear glass shade gives a warm, omnidirectional glow that reads as both practical (enough to read by) and atmospheric (warm enough to feel like evening, even in the day). The copper fitting adds a warm metallic note that ties the whole material palette together.

## Interior tips

- **Layer your cushion textures**: the combination of linen, sheepskin, leather and macramé in this chair is deliberate. Mixing materials makes the arrangement look collected and personal rather than bought as a set.
- **A trailing plant on a wall hook** beside the chair adds life and softness to what would otherwise be a hard corner. Pothos, string of pearls and heartleaf philodendron all trail beautifully and tolerate the lower light of a corner.
- **Use a vintage rug rather than a plain one**: patterned vintage rugs in faded, muted tones add age and depth to the floor without demanding attention. They feel earned rather than purchased.
- **Keep the shelf styling minimal**: with a hanging chair and globe pendant already in the space, the shelves should recede. A few books, one framed print, one candle. Anything more competes.
- **Leave the nook tech-free**: no phone charger cable, no speaker, no screen. This is the most powerful way to make a reading nook actually become a reading nook — if it looks like a reading chair, it will be used as one.
