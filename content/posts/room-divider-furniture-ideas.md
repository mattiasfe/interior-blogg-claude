This piece of furniture is almost certainly handmade — the kind of project someone built on a weekend with salvaged pallet timber, some screws, a jigsaw and a plan drawn on paper. The result is a reading bench with open bookshelf cubbies running underneath the seat and a tall shelf tower rising at one end. Both sections are full of books — colourful spines, different sizes, clearly used and loved. The seat has a long padded cushion in natural linen and two decorative pillows — one with a floral print, one with a stripe. The whole piece sits against a window, with plants on the windowsill and a patterned rug below. It is practical, characterful and entirely homemade.

## Why pallet wood works for a bookshelf bench

Pallet timber is widely available, inexpensive or free, and comes pre-aged — the rough surface, visible grain and occasional marking are what make it look intentional rather than cheap. The boards are consistent in thickness, which makes joining them straightforward. And the natural variation between individual boards (slightly different tones, different grain directions) creates the visual interest that a factory-cut shelf piece would lack.

## The structure: bench and tower

The bench section is a simple platform — a rectangular base frame of upright boards, with horizontal shelves at two heights creating open cubbies across the full width. These cubbies hold books in landscape orientation (stacked flat) or standing upright. The seat surface sits directly on top, with no additional framework needed. The tower at one end is built the same way: uprights with horizontal shelves at approximately 30 cm spacing. A flat top on the tower provides a surface for a plant pot.

## The seat cushion

The cushion is the element that turns this from a shelf into a piece of furniture you want to sit on. A simple platform cushion — foam core cut to the bench dimensions, covered in natural linen or cotton — is enough. The thickness should be at least 8–10 cm for genuine comfort; thinner than this and you can feel the timber underneath. Linen covers in natural or cream look right on raw timber.

## What to store and display

Books on all the shelves — this is a bookshelf bench, not a display cabinet. The more books, the better: a half-full bookshelf on this kind of piece reads as unfinished. Additional items visible here include a framed photo on the tower, a small ceramic piece and a potted plant on top. These are additions to a book collection, not substitutes for it.

## Interior tips

- **Alternate the book orientation**: some stacked flat (spines up), some standing upright. This variation is more interesting than uniformly upright books, and allows you to use the flat stacks as surfaces for small objects.
- **Leave the wood unfinished or oil it**: a clear wood oil will bring out the natural colour and grain of pallet timber without looking like a furniture-shop finish. Varnish or paint would lose the rustic quality entirely.
- **Position it under a window if possible**: the daylight above the bench makes reading on it practical, and the window behind plants creates the layered look visible here.
- **A patterned vintage rug below** adds colour to a piece that is entirely in natural wood tones. The rug is where the pattern lives; everything else stays quiet.
- **Fill the shelves completely**: an overfull bookshelf looks generous and well-used. An underfull one looks abandoned. If you don't have enough books yet, borrow some from other rooms while you build the collection.
