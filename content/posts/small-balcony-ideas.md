This hallway has found the most inventive use for an old door: it's been hung on its original hinges so that when it opens into the corner of the hallway, its inner face reveals three wooden shelves fitted at its back. The shelves are shallow — just deep enough for a glass oil lamp, a ceramic flower, a glass vase with cut hydrangeas and magnolias — and they're stained in the same whitewash as the door itself. The door is antique, heavily distressed, the paint worn to bare wood in long vertical streaks. A vintage door knob with its original keyhole plate remains in place. At the base of the door, wooden block letters spell FARM. The door swings open and your hallway becomes a display cabinet.

## The concept: a door that becomes a shelf

The mechanism here is simple: the door opens against the wall and the corner catches it, holding it at approximately 90 degrees. The shelves fitted to the interior face of the door are now accessible and stable. When the door closes, the shelves are hidden. This is a display nook that can be concealed or revealed with one motion — and it requires no drilling into walls, no built-in construction, just a door and some shelf brackets.

## Fitting the shelves

The shelves are cut from the same timber as the door and fitted using small shelf brackets screwed directly into the door planks. Because the shelves are lightweight and decorative (not designed to hold heavy items), the brackets can be small and the loads are minimal. The shelves sit at three heights that align with the door's natural panel divisions, reinforcing the vertical rhythm of the piece.

## The distressed whitewash finish

The door and shelves here share a unified finish: a heavily worn whitewash, applied over original paint and then aged through use, exposure or deliberate sanding. The streaks of bare timber showing through the white — running vertically with the grain — create the characteristic look of old painted outdoor furniture left to weather. To replicate this, apply a diluted white paint wash (one part paint, one part water) over bare or lightly sanded wood, let it dry, then sand heavily in the direction of the grain.

## What to display

The shelves here hold objects with the same aged, natural quality as the door itself: a glass oil lamp (amber glass, brass fittings), a dried rust-coloured magnolia bloom in a small glass vessel, a large glass vase with white hydrangeas and dark magnolia leaves. Everything is either antique, dried, or clear glass. Nothing is painted a primary colour; nothing is new and shiny. The styling principle is consistency of era and material.

## Interior tips

- **Position the door against a corner wall rather than a flat wall**: a corner catches the door and holds it at a stable 90 degrees. Against a flat wall, you'd need a door stop, which is less elegant.
- **Use only lightweight objects on the door shelves**: the shelves are screwed into a door, not a wall. They will hold small ceramics, glass vessels and dried botanicals, but not heavy books or stacked plates.
- **Keep the same finish on door and shelves**: the unity of the whitewash across both elements is what makes the piece look designed. Mismatched finishes would make the shelves look added rather than integrated.
- **Dried botanicals rather than fresh**: fresh flowers need water (impractical on a shelf) and wilt within days. Dried flowers last indefinitely and have the same hushed, aged quality as the door itself.
- **One line of text at the base**: the FARM letters here complete the farmhouse narrative without overstating it. One word is enough; a full phrase would be too much.
