This studio apartment is the opposite of minimal — and it's all the better for it. A tall wooden bookshelf packed with books and plants and small objects stands between the kitchen (white cabinets, stainless appliances, a small kitchen island) and the living area (cream sofa, ethnic cushions in rust and navy and terracotta). On the floor: a large Persian-style rug in teal, rust and cream. On the coffee table: a candle in a glass holder, a small trailing plant. On the right wall near the bed: a gallery of framed botanical prints and photographs with string lights strung above them. The bed has grey bedding with rust-orange accent cushions. Everything in this apartment is warm, layered and personal. This is what a home looks like when someone actually lives in it.

## The bookshelf as the room's spine

The tall wooden shelf running between the kitchen and living zones is not a neutral piece of furniture — it's the room's spine. Fully loaded with books of various heights, interspersed with trailing plants, ceramic pieces and small framed photos, it reads as a personal collection rather than a styled display. Books here are not arranged by colour or height; they are arranged by use, which is actually more honest and more visually interesting. The shelf provides a backdrop for both zones: on the kitchen side, it completes the room; on the living side, it creates the wall that the sofa needs behind it.

## The Persian rug as the living room floor

A large Persian or Persian-style rug in deep teal, rust and cream covers the living zone completely. This is the single most impactful element in the room. The rug's age and pattern depth add a quality of history and warmth that no other object in the room could match. In a studio apartment where the "living room" exists only as a conceptual zone rather than a physical room, a rug this large and this characterful performs the function of walls — it makes the living area feel like a room.

## String lights above the gallery wall

The string lights strung above the gallery wall near the bed are a decision that transforms the room in the evening. Rather than using conventional bedside lighting, this apartment uses a constellation of warm LED dots to create an ambient glow that backlights the art and suffuses the sleeping zone with warm, even light. It's the same principle as candles — soft, omnidirectional, not pointing at anything specifically.

## The small kitchen island

The kitchen section is visible in the background: white upper cabinets, stainless steel appliances (refrigerator, range and microwave), and a small white kitchen island providing extra worktop and storage. The island's footprint is minimal but its contribution is significant — it creates a clear boundary between kitchen and living zone at floor level, reinforcing the division that the bookshelf creates at eye level.

## Interior tips

- **A full bookshelf looks alive**: the shelves here are dense with books, and it's this density that creates the warmth. A half-full shelf looks hesitant; a shelf loaded with books that have actually been read looks like someone lives there.
- **Let your rug set the room's colour palette**: the teal, rust and cream of the Persian rug is echoed in the cushions, the candlelight and the small objects throughout the room. Start with the rug and choose everything else from it.
- **String lights are not a teenage bedroom choice**: used thoughtfully, above a gallery wall or draped across a window, string lights are one of the most effective warm light sources available. LED string lights at 2700K are perfect.
- **Ethnic cushions need no matching**: the mix of rust block-print, navy geometric and cream textured cushions on the sofa here is deliberately varied. The unifying factor is the warm-toned palette, not any pattern match.
- **A kitchen island visible from the living room** should be styled as furniture, not just as a worktop. A small plant, a clean jar or a wooden cutting board on display treats the island as part of the home, not just as a kitchen surface.
