Standing in the hallway of this apartment and looking in, three distinct spaces reveal themselves simultaneously. On the left: the bedroom, its back wall painted a calm sage green, a framed landscape photograph above the bed, a bedside table with a softly glowing candle. In the centre: a tall open oak bookshelf serving as the room divider, its shelves holding books, woven storage boxes, plants and decorative objects. On the right: the living zone — a forest green fabric sofa, a dark round coffee table with a small plant tray, a cream accent armchair, an arc floor lamp with a pleated drum shade. Further to the right: a wooden desk with a laptop and desk lamp positioned against the large city-view window. This is a studio apartment that has been divided into three coherent, functional spaces without a single wall being built.

## The sage green bedroom wall

The back wall of the bedroom is painted in a deep, muted sage green — the darkest element in an otherwise neutral room, and the most deliberate. This single painted wall does the work of an entire room: it defines the sleeping area as a separate zone, creates a mood distinct from the living space, and gives the bed an anchored, finished quality it wouldn't have against a plain white wall. One dark feature wall in a studio apartment is one of the most effective and least reversible ways to create genuine spatial division.

## The bookshelf as divider

The tall open oak shelf positioned between the bedroom and living zone is not so tall that it blocks light, and not so open that it fails to define zones. Its shelves are organised intentionally: books at the bottom, woven rattan storage boxes in the middle, plants and decorative objects at the top. The mix of storage and display means the shelf works from both sides — it contributes to both the bedroom and the living zone it divides.

## The living zone

On the living room side of the divider: a deep forest green fabric sofa with cream cushions, a round dark wooden coffee table on hairpin legs with a tray holding a small plant and a candle, and a cream armchair. The arc floor lamp — with a tall curved arm and a pleated drum shade — provides reading light over the sofa and adds the vertical element that the low furniture doesn't. This zone has everything a living room needs in the footprint of a corner.

## The desk by the window

The wooden desk positioned against the large window has the city view and natural light that any home office needs. A functional black desk chair, a desk lamp, a few plants on the windowsill. The choice to put the workspace at the window is practical and visually smart: it takes the zone that needs the most light and gives it the most light, while the bedroom (which benefits from darkness) is positioned further from the main window.

## Interior tips

- **Paint one wall of the bedroom zone in a deep, muted tone**: sage green, navy, warm charcoal, or deep terracotta. The colour creates a room without building a room. It signals to anyone in the apartment that this is a different space.
- **Match the divider shelf material to the desk**: both are oak here. This repetition across two functional pieces — one a divider, one a workspace — creates material coherence across the whole studio.
- **A round coffee table on hairpin legs** takes up less visual floor space than a rectangular table and allows you to move around it freely from any direction. In a studio where every path matters, this is significant.
- **An arc floor lamp reaches over the sofa** without occupying sofa-side floor space. The lamp base sits behind the sofa or at the end of it, and the arched arm positions the shade above the seating area. This is the solution for a living zone without room for a side table lamp.
- **Plants in every zone**: a bedside plant, a plant on the bookshelf divider, a coffee table plant, a windowsill plant. Each zone gets at least one living thing. It ties the zones together while giving each its own life.
