Someone took an old panelled door — the kind with deep rectangular mouldings, painted long ago in a dusty teal-turquoise that has since worn through to bare wood in patches — and fitted it with horizontal shelves. The lower half of the door, where the original panels form a natural cabinet shape, has been left intact. The upper half opens up to display shelves holding plants in white ceramic pots, stacked books, a small framed photo and a few quiet objects. The original black door handles are still in place. It leans against the wall rather than being wall-mounted — held upright by its own weight and the furniture around it. It is entirely original and deeply satisfying.

## Why a door works as a bookshelf

The proportions of a standard interior door are almost exactly right for a tall, narrow bookshelf. The depth of the door frame provides a natural ledge; the height is typically 2 metres, which fills a wall without overwhelming it. A door's panelled face gives each shelf a natural frame — the recessed panels below become the cabinet section, and the flat face above becomes the display surface.

## What to look for

Reclamation yards, architectural salvage shops and online marketplaces (Finn.no, Facebook Marketplace, eBay) are the best sources for old doors. Look for solid wood doors rather than hollow-core ones — the weight provides stability when leaning, and the material accepts shelving hardware well. Panelled doors in distressed paint are the most characterful; the more layers of old paint showing through, the better.

## Fitting the shelves

The simplest approach: horizontal timber planks cut to the internal width of the door frame, supported by metal shelf brackets screwed directly into the door's vertical stiles. Use short screws to avoid breaking through the face. Space the shelves at book height — approximately 30–35 cm apart — to give flexibility for different book sizes and plant heights.

## Styling the result

The styling here is restrained and very effective. White ceramic pots in different sizes hold small green plants — succulents, ferns, small foliage plants. Books are stacked horizontally in blocks rather than spine-out, which reads more as a display than a library. A small framed portrait photograph leans against the back of one shelf. The objects chosen have a quiet, slightly vintage character that complements the distressed paint.

## Interior tips

- **Don't repaint the distressed surface**: the layered, worn paint is the whole point. Clean it, wax it if it's dry, leave the rest. Painting over it would destroy what makes it interesting.
- **Use white or cream ceramic pots**: the contrast between bright white ceramics and the weathered teal paint is extremely effective. Terracotta would work too; avoid plastic pots.
- **Keep the original hardware**: the black door handles are part of the piece's character. If you remove them, it becomes just a bookshelf. With them, it's a door that has become a bookshelf — a completely different thing.
- **Let it lean rather than mounting it**: leaning furniture has a casual, confident quality that mounted furniture doesn't. It also means no drilling, and the piece can be moved easily.
- **Stack some books horizontally**: a mix of upright spines and horizontal stacks creates a more interesting shelf silhouette than all-vertical books, and the flat stacks provide a surface for small objects.
