Beside a grey sofa draped with a textured throw, a freestanding S-shaped bookshelf table in natural oak holds exactly the things that usually end up scattered across a sofa or piled on a floor: books, a mug, a small glass vase with a single white ranunculus and dried lavender stems, a wicker basket of wrapped chocolates. The shelf's form is immediately distinctive — three curved semicircular shelves arranged in an S-shape, each level rotating 180 degrees relative to the one below it, giving the piece an organic, sculptural quality that no conventional rectangular shelf could achieve. The natural oak grain is visible throughout. It occupies the floor space of a large dinner plate.

## What makes the S-curve form work

A conventional side table has one surface at one height. This S-curve shelf has three surfaces at three heights — the top at arm height (for the mug and vase), the middle at a lower reach (for current reading), the bottom at floor level (for a basket or overflow). This vertical distribution of storage is more useful than a single flat surface and more elegant than a conventional bookcase. The curved form means the shelf presents something interesting from every angle — it's sculptural from the side, functional from the sofa, and surprising from across the room.

## The three shelves and what goes on each

**Top shelf**: the surface in use. A glass vase with a fresh white ranunculus and dried lavender, a black ceramic mug of coffee or tea. This is what you need within reach right now.

**Middle shelf**: current reading. Books read front-cover-out or spine-up — The Alchemist, Dune — and a couple of magazines (Monocle, a design publication). These are books being read, not books being stored.

**Bottom shelf**: the basket. A wicker basket with whatever accumulates — in this case, wrapped chocolates, but it could be a phone charger, a TV remote, reading glasses, a notebook. The basket contains the miscellaneous without exposing it.

## Natural oak: the right material

The choice of natural, unfinished or lightly oiled oak is correct for a piece this organic in form. Dark stained wood would make the S-curve feel heavy and formal; painted wood would lose the grain that gives the form warmth. Raw or lightly oiled oak at this scale is the same material language as a turned wooden bowl or a carved breadboard — honest, warm, handmade-feeling even when it isn't.

## Where to place it

Beside the sofa, within arm's reach from a seated position. The top shelf should sit at approximately the same height as the sofa's arm — this is the surface you reach without looking. The piece needs open floor around it to be seen from the side, which is where its form is most visible. Don't push it into a corner or against a wall.

## Interior tips

- **Put the thing you use most at the top**: your mug, your current book, your phone. The top of the S-shelf is the most accessible point; put the thing you reach for most frequently there, not the most decorative item.
- **One fresh flower and one dried element** in the same small vase (the white ranunculus and dried lavender here) is more interesting than all-fresh or all-dried. The contrast between the living and the preserved is visually compelling.
- **A wicker basket at the bottom shelf** is the right container for miscellaneous items that don't have a home. The wicker is warm in tone and material, matching the oak. A plastic bin or fabric bag would look wrong.
- **The shelf works best beside a sofa, not behind a sofa**: this piece is designed to be seen from the sofa and reached from the sofa. Position it accordingly.
- **Current books only**: the middle shelf of the S-curve holds books in use, not books in storage. If a book has been read and returned to the shelf but is just taking up space, move it to a bookcase. This shelf is for what you're reading now, and that distinction matters.
