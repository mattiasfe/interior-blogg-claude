This room is pure hygge. A stone fireplace with exposed plaster surround is lit, the orange glow of the fire visible through the opening. Beside it, a stack of cut logs reaches from floor to mantle. The white deep sofa is layered with chunky knit cushions in cream and natural, a sheepskin throw, a linen blanket. A rustic wooden stool — the kind made from a cross-section of a log — serves as a coffee table, holding a small dish and two eggs. A massive chunky-knit rug in off-white covers the floor. Dried botanicals and bare branches sit in ceramic vessels on the mantle. In the corner: a dried pampas plume. Through the window to the right, a grey winter sky.

## The fireplace as the room's heart

Everything in this room is oriented around the fireplace. The sofa faces it. The log stack frames it. The mantle display echoes it. A working fireplace is the ultimate Scandinavian interior feature — not just for warmth but for light, for sound and for the way it anchors a room around a single point of focus. If you have a working fireplace, let it be the most important thing in the room.

## Stacked logs as a design element

The stack of firewood beside the hearth here is not hidden, not stored in a basket, not tucked out of sight. It's displayed — full height, neatly stacked, filling the recess from floor to mantle. Firewood is a beautiful material: the cross-sections of cut logs have a graphic circular pattern that's both natural and geometric. An integrated log stack signals that the fireplace is used, that the house is warm, and that practical things can also be beautiful.

## The sofa: a cloud of natural fibres

The sofa here is white or near-white — a deep, low-sitting piece that appears to be entirely covered in soft furnishings rather than the structural sofa itself. Chunky cable-knit cushions in cream and natural, a sheepskin draped over one arm, a heavy linen throw folded at the end. The effect is a sofa that looks like you could disappear into it. The key is the consistency of colour: everything is within a very narrow range of white, cream and natural, so the different textures read as one warm, layered composition rather than a collection of separate throws.

## The rustic wooden stool coffee table

The coffee table here is a cross-section of a thick tree trunk or a rough-hewn wooden stool — extremely low, extremely rustic, and perfect in this context. It holds almost nothing: a small ceramic dish with two eggs. The choice to use almost nothing on the table is what makes the rest of the room's softness so impactful — the hard, raw surface of the wood provides the contrast that makes the textiles around it feel even softer.

## Interior tips

- **Stack your logs high**: a half-full log stack looks inadequate and unintentional. A floor-to-ceiling stack reads as deliberate and generous. Buy more than you need and let the stack be a feature.
- **Chunky knit in natural colours only**: the knit items here — cushions, rug — are all undyed or very lightly dyed natural fibre. The moment you introduce a bright or saturated colour, the hygge mood breaks. Stay within a palette of cream, oatmeal, grey and warm white.
- **A dried botanical arrangement on the mantle** is more appropriate than fresh flowers in this context. Dried seed heads, pampas grass and bare branches have the quietness and restraint of winter — they belong in a room like this.
- **The floor cushion matters**: the large floor cushion visible in the foreground turns the floor into seating — a very Scandinavian instinct. It signals that the room is used, that people sit everywhere, that the space is genuinely lived in.
- **Let the fire be the only light source in the evening**: turn off all electric lights and let the room be lit entirely by the flames. No other lighting decision will have the same effect on atmosphere.
